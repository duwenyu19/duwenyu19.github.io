// professional bio

About me

I am a graduate student in Indiana Wesleyan University's AI/ML program and a software engineer. Before this program, I helped my company build an in-house LLM that turned customers' plain-language questions into SQL queries against our internal databases and returned the answers — no analyst in the loop. Our database had about 1,500 tables, and our first attempt at training the model on the full schema failed: it was too slow and demanded hardware we didn't have. The version that worked was narrower and smarter — a curated set of high-relevance tables and their relationships, a vector database retrieving the right schema context per request, and verified correct answers fed back into training.

That project shaped what I believe about this field: the model is rarely the hard part. Choosing the right data, setting honest expectations, and bringing people along are what determine whether AI actually delivers. I'm in this program to build the leadership half of that equation.

// personal value proposition

What I offer

I bridge the two halves of AI adoption that usually live in different people: I have shipped an LLM system in production, so I know where these projects really fail — data quality, scope, and expectations — and I am deliberately building the change-leadership skills to guide teams through adoption. Organizations don't just need someone who can build the model or someone who can manage the change. I'm working to be the person who can do both, and this portfolio documents that journey from the first week.

artifact_01 · visual research

The Evolution of AI: A Visual Timeline (1950–2026)

title
The Evolution of AI: A Visual Timeline (1950–2026)
introduction
A designed, eight-slide visual timeline tracing artificial intelligence from Alan Turing's 1950 question to today's generative AI race — including the two "AI winters" that nearly killed the field.
description
The deck opens with a seven-decade overview timeline, then dedicates a slide to each era: the birth of AI (1950–1969), the two AI winters and the expert-system boom (1970–1995), the machine learning and cloud comeback (1997–2011), the deep learning revolution (2012–2019), and the generative AI era (2020–present), closing with sources. Each milestone carries a plain-language explanation of why it mattered, and the AI winters are annotated with their causes and impacts.
objective
To understand — and help others understand — how AI actually developed: not as a straight line of progress, but as cycles of hype, collapse, and breakthrough driven as much by hardware and data as by algorithms.
process
I researched milestones across three source types (a documentary video, an IEEE overview, and Our World in Data's compute-growth analysis), selected the events that best explain each era's turning point, organized them chronologically, and designed the deck with a dark circuit-board visual theme, color-coding eras and using an alternating node layout for the overview timeline.
tools and technologies used
Microsoft PowerPoint for the final deck; Claude (Anthropic) for research assistance and milestone organization; sources listed below.
value proposition
This artifact shows I can research a complex technical history, judge which details matter, and communicate them visually to a non-technical audience — the same skill needed to brief stakeholders on any AI initiative.
unique value
Most AI timelines celebrate the wins. Mine gives equal weight to the failures — the winters — because as someone who has trained an LLM, I know overpromising is the field's oldest failure mode, and the leaders who remember it make better decisions.
relevance
For hiring managers and collaborators: evidence of research synthesis, visual communication, and historical judgment about AI hype cycles — directly applicable to technology strategy and stakeholder education.
references
  • Putchuon – "Put You On." (2024). The entire history of artificial intelligence (Last 100 years) [Video]. YouTube.
  • IEEE Computer Society. (n.d.). The evolution of AI: From foundations to future prospects.
  • Roser, M., et al. (n.d.). A brief history of artificial intelligence. Our World in Data.

artifact_02 · analytical writing

Machine Learning vs. Deep Learning: Matching the Approach to the Problem

title
Machine Learning vs. Deep Learning: Matching the Approach to the Problem in Two Real-World Applications
introduction
A professional comparative-analysis report examining two real-world AI applications — regression-based property valuation and Transformer-based machine translation — and arguing, for each, not only why its approach fits but why the opposite approach would fail. Where my first artifact shows visual storytelling, this one shows a different skill: structured analytical writing backed by research.
description
The report analyzes house price prediction as a traditional ML problem, using the regression-based computer-assisted mass appraisal (CAMA) systems that county assessors run on hundreds of thousands of properties, and machine translation as a deep learning problem, using Google Translate's 2016 shift from statistical to neural translation. Each case gets a two-way argument: four reasons the chosen approach fits, and the structural reasons the opposite one does not. A personal section connects the valuation example to China's ongoing housing correction — where prices have fallen for 35 consecutive months and inflation-adjusted values have dropped below their 2005 level — and asks why economists with models saw it coming while ordinary families could not.
objective
To build, and demonstrate, the judgment that AI leadership actually requires: not mastery of the most advanced technique, but the fit analysis that decides when a simple interpretable model beats a deep network — and when it cannot.
process
I selected one traditional ML example and one deep learning example from the course lesson, researched a verifiable real-world deployment of each, built the double-sided suitability arguments along four dimensions (data structure, data volume, interpretability requirements, computational cost), then added the China housing analysis using current market data and the pre-crisis academic warnings, and structured everything as a professional report in APA style.
tools and technologies used
Microsoft Word for the report; web research across market data (NBS, BIS) and academic sources; Claude (Anthropic) for drafting and organization assistance, disclosed in the report itself; APA 7 citation format.
value proposition
This artifact proves I can argue both directions of a technical decision — the rarer skill. Anyone can explain why deep learning is powerful; explaining precisely why it is the wrong tool for a small, regulated, tabular problem is what separates technology selection from technology enthusiasm.
unique value
The China housing section is analysis no template produces: it connects model choice to who gets access to insight — professionals with models foresaw the correction, families without them could not — and closes with a caution I learned shipping an LLM in production: a model is only as far-sighted as the features it is given.
relevance
For employers and collaborators: evidence of technical writing, research synthesis, and framework-driven decision-making about when (and when not) to deploy deep learning — directly applicable to architecture reviews, vendor evaluation, and AI strategy.
references
  • Rogoff, K. S., & Yang, Y. (2021). Has China's housing production peaked? China & World Economy, 29(1), 1–31.
  • Vaswani, A., et al. (2017). Attention is all you need. NeurIPS 30.
  • Wu, Y., et al. (2016). Google's neural machine translation system. arXiv.
  • International Association of Assessing Officers. (2017). Standard on mass appraisal of real property.

artifact_03 · applied ai · team project

"Walk the Talk — Speakers Bureau" AI Booking Bot

title
“Walk the Talk — Speakers Bureau” AI Booking Agent (Team Project)
introduction
A working AI booking portal, built with my team in Google AI Studio, that matches event organizers with speakers by topic, audience, language, and budget — combining a filterable speaker directory, a side-by-side comparison portal, and a conversational AI booking agent.
description
The project ran in five phases: deep-dive peer interviews to uncover each team member's real story; AI-drafted TED-talk-style introductions; professional speaker profiles (bio, keynote topics, speaking style, A/V needs, pricing); team execution split across three task forces — bot building, a process infographic, and a marketing infographic; and a final live demo answering unscripted audience queries. The finished portal loads eight real speaker profiles and runs in "sole authority mode": the agent answers strictly from the official speaker-profiles document. Asked "Who speaks Chinese?", it correctly identifies the only Chinese-speaking speaker on the roster (me) instead of inventing an answer — evidence that the grounding worked.
objective
To experience the full lifecycle of a small human-centered AI product — from gathering real human input, to constraining model behavior with a system prompt, to shipping something a stranger can actually use.
process
Interview teammates and take structured notes → generate and iterate speaker introductions with AI → build speaker profiles → upload content to Google AI Studio and author the system prompt → test and refine against off-script queries → produce the process and marketing infographics → present with a live unscripted demo.
tools and technologies used
Google AI Studio (bot platform and system prompt), generative AI tools for infographic design and copy iteration, team collaboration for content and testing.
value proposition
This artifact proves I can take AI beyond the chat window: constraining a model with a system prompt, structuring real content for retrieval, and testing behavior against unpredictable users are the exact skills production chatbots require.
unique value
Having built a production LLM system before, I pushed the team to treat the system prompt like an engineering spec — strict scope, defined refusals, structured data — rather than a suggestion. The bot stayed on-task during the live unscripted demo because of it.
relevance
For employers exploring customer-facing AI: a compact, verifiable demonstration of conversational AI design, prompt engineering discipline, and human-centered process — from interview to launch.
references
Built with Google AI Studio. Infographics created by our team's task forces as project deliverables.

artifact_04 · leadership baseline

AI Leadership Baseline: Self-Assessment, Visualized

title
AI Leadership Baseline: Self-Assessment, Visualized
introduction
Radar charts visualizing my scores from two professional self-assessments — Change Management Skills and AI/ML Integration Leadership Skills — taken in the first week of my graduate program, published as an honest baseline to measure my growth against.
description
Each chart plots my self-assessed score in every category as a percentage of that category's maximum, making strengths and gaps visible at a glance. The shape tells the story: technical categories (Critical Thinking 90%, Technical Understanding of AI/ML 90%, Problem-Solving 84%) run high, while people-side categories (Change Readiness 45%, Change Management Knowledge 47%, Leadership through transitions 48%) run low.
objective
To establish a measurable starting point for my leadership development — and to practice, on myself, the same discipline I'd bring to any AI project: measure honestly before you optimize.
process
I completed both assessments item by item, scoring conservatively rather than generously; totaled each category; converted totals to percentages so categories of different sizes could be compared; and plotted them as radar charts. I then critiqued the instruments themselves — redundant items, inconsistent weighting, pure self-report — and proposed revisions in a peer discussion.
tools and technologies used
Microsoft Word (assessment forms), Python with Matplotlib (data visualization), course readings from the Center for Creative Leadership and Change Strategists to frame the analysis.
value proposition
This artifact demonstrates two things at once: I can turn raw self-evaluation data into a clear visualization, and I have the self-awareness to publish my weaknesses next to my strengths — the trait research says change leaders need most.
unique value
Most portfolios only show finished strengths. This one shows the gap analysis — the same radar shape a technical leader would draw for a system audit, applied to myself. Revisiting these charts at the end of the program will make my growth verifiable, not just claimed.
relevance
For employers and mentors: evidence of data visualization skills, honest self-evaluation, and a concrete professional development plan — I know exactly which skills I'm building and why.
references
  • Center for Creative Leadership. (2024). How to be a successful change leader.
  • Change Strategists. (2023). How to assess change management skills.
